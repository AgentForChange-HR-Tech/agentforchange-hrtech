Platform selection framework
Key evaluation dimensions
Agent depth
Can agents execute work end-to-end, or just surface recommendations? True agentic capability requires bidirectional system access, not just read.
Orchestration
Can the platform coordinate agents across multiple systems (HRIS, ATS, LMS, payroll)? Cross-system orchestration is the key differentiator in 2026.
Governance
Does every agentic action generate an audit trail? Human-override capability? Guardrails that enforce company rules — not just suggest them?
Context layer
Does the platform have a semantic layer — company policies, career frameworks, org structure — that agents can tap? Context is what creates value.
Open standards
MCP (Model Context Protocol) and A2A support enables interoperability with external agents. Vendor lock-in risk is highest when these are absent.
Pricing model
AI included vs. AI à la carte. ServiceNow's "included" model is raising the bar. Consumption pricing adds unpredictability at scale.

The Bersin 5-Layer Agent Architecture
Layer 1 — Systems of Record
Workday, SAP, Oracle, UKG. The foundational data layer — employee records, org structure, payroll, benefits. The "rails" that give agents context and authority to act.
Layer 2 — Workflow & Experience
ServiceNow, Microsoft Viva, internal portals. Cross-system coordination — making disparate systems work together. The orchestration layer that connects what lives above and below.
Layer 3 — Individual Agents
Functional AI agents that maintain intelligence and act on your behalf. Recruiting agent, onboarding agent, benefits agent, performance agent. Each executes a specific domain end-to-end.
Layer 4 — Superagents
AI systems that stitch together multiple functional agents to manage entire HR processes. A Talent Superagent might coordinate recruiting, onboarding, and L&D agents in sequence — no human handoffs.
Layer 5 — Agent Platforms & Studios
Galileo (Bersin), Microsoft Copilot Studio, Oracle Agent Studio, SAP Joule Studio, Sana (Workday). The development and management environments where agents are built, deployed, and governed.
Core concepts every practitioner needs to know
Agentic AI vs. Copilot AI
A copilot assists humans — it surfaces suggestions and waits for approval. An agent acts autonomously — it executes multi-step workflows, coordinates across systems, and completes tasks end-to-end. The distinction matters enormously for how you design processes and governance.
Systemic HR
Bersin's foundational model for 2026. HR as an integrated operation — not siloed Centers of Excellence. Talent acquisition, L&D, performance, and compensation connected through shared data and agent infrastructure rather than separate tools and teams.
Superworker
The human + AI collaboration model where individuals are augmented by agents to perform at dramatically higher levels. A recruiter with a sourcing agent, screening agent, and scheduling agent isn't one person — they're effectively a team.
MCP — Model Context Protocol
Open standard for agent interoperability — enables AI agents from different vendors to share context and work together. Workday's Agent Gateway and ServiceNow both support MCP. Increasingly a procurement requirement for enterprise HR platforms.
Semantic Layer / Context Engineering
"Everyone is ignoring the big boring problem of bad context." Context is what creates value — if we can't trust the content, we can't trust the agent. The semantic layer maintains company policies, career frameworks, and org rules that agents reference to make accurate decisions.

Glossary — terms to know
HCM
Human Capital Management — the category of enterprise software covering core HR, payroll, benefits, talent management.
HRIS
Human Resource Information System — the system of record for employee data. Often used interchangeably with HCM.
ATS
Applicant Tracking System — manages recruiting workflows from requisition to offer. Workday Recruiting, Greenhouse, Lever, iCIMS.
LXP
Learning Experience Platform — AI-personalized learning delivery. Sana (Workday), Cornerstone, Degreed.
MCP
Model Context Protocol — open standard for agent interoperability across vendor systems.
RAG
Retrieval Augmented Generation — technique that grounds AI outputs in company-specific data, reducing hallucinations in enterprise HR agents.
Why tech companies face the most acute HR transformation pressure
The Specialized Talent War
Global Challenge

Semiconductor, hardware, and AI infrastructure companies compete for some of the scarcest talent in the global economy. Chip design engineers take 5–7 years to fully develop. AI silicon architects are being recruited by every major technology company simultaneously. The talent supply is structurally limited against demand driven by the AI infrastructure boom — and the companies that win this talent war will win the next decade of technology leadership.

5–7yr engineer developmentGlobal talent scarcityAI infrastructure demand
The Compensation Benchmark Effect
Market Dynamic

Nvidia's stock-driven compensation packages have created a global benchmark that most technology companies struggle to match on cash alone. This forces HR leaders to compete on total value proposition — culture, mission, growth trajectory, flexibility, and non-cash benefits — rather than pure compensation. AI-powered compensation modeling and market intelligence tools are becoming critical infrastructure for any tech company's talent retention strategy.

Nvidia benchmark effectTotal value propositionComp modeling critical
Skills Evolution Speed
L&D Priority

AI silicon, PCIe 6.0, next-gen networking protocols, and custom ASIC design require constant upskilling. L&D programs built for stable skill sets are structurally inadequate for hardware engineering environments where the technology landscape shifts every 18–24 months. Skills intelligence infrastructure — tracking what skills exist internally, what skills the market demands, and how fast skills are being acquired — is a competitive requirement, not a nice-to-have.

18–24mo skill cyclesInternal skills mappingAcquisition velocity
Immigration Complexity at Scale
Operational Reality

Technology companies — particularly those in semiconductor and hardware — have among the highest concentrations of H-1B sponsored employees in the US workforce. The $100K supplemental fee, wage-weighted lottery, and EAD auto-extension elimination create material financial and operational risks that HR must model into workforce planning. Immigration is not an administrative function in tech — it is a strategic talent pipeline management challenge.

High H-1B concentration$100K fee impactStrategic pipeline risk

The HR opportunity in tech
Now
AI as the equalizer
Companies that operationalize AI-driven talent intelligence first will win the talent war. HR has a rare chance to lead this transformation — not follow it.
Recruiting
AI agents for specialized sourcing
Sourcing agents can identify candidates with niche hardware skills at scale — reducing time-to-hire for roles that historically took 6+ months to fill.
Retention
Predictive flight risk modeling
Identifying at-risk engineers 3–6 months before resignation is worth hundreds of thousands per prevented departure in specialized roles.
Onboarding
Faster time-to-productivity
Superagents reduce onboarding complexity in technical environments — provisioning, training, team integration — from weeks to days.
Planning
Workforce intelligence as strategy
Monitoring competitor hiring patterns surfaces market signals 6–12 months before they show up in financial results. That lead time is the advantage.
